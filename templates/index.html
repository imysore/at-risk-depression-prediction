Are you at risk for depression?

Introduction

In this digital age, the prevalence of social media platforms in adolescents' lives is linked with a rise in depression. Media influence and gender norms can worsen the discrepancy between adolescents' actual experiences and their perceived notions for the future. This causes adolescents to have inadequate perceptions of themselves. Common obstacles to effective care, the WHO reports, include “a lack of investment in mental health care, lack of trained health-care providers and social stigma associated with mental disorders.” Failure to address depression during the adolescent stages may result in more severe consequences toward adulthood, impairing mental health and limiting adults from achieving their full potential.

Did you know that...

  • Over 17% of adolescents experience symptoms of depression.
  • Over 20% of adolescents diagnosed with a mental health disorder don't receive the necessary support.
  • The risk of depression increases by 13% for each hour on social media.
  • Over 75% of individuals in low- and middle-income countries who have a mental health condition do not receive treatment.

You are not alone

Make sure your account is public.

This application should never be used as a substitute for professional medical evaluation or treatment. The predictions made by this model are probabilistic and should be used only to highlight the need for a more thorough evaluation by a healthcare provider.

How does this work?

  • This application will analyze your Twitter (X) or Instagram social media account.
  • Using a machine learning model that's been trained on half a million depressed and nondepressed social media posts, the application will assess the frequency of posts in your account that contain elements of depression.
  • Based on this assessment, the application will predict if you are at risk for depression. If necessary, the application will lead you to some resources or suggest that you seek an actual diagnosis from a mental health professional.
